Post-Stroke Gait Rehabilitation

The most evidence-rich indication for lower-limb exoskeletons. A 2023 Cochrane meta-analysis of 36 RCTs confirmed robot-assisted gait training significantly improves walking speed, endurance and independence versus conventional physiotherapy. Effect is strongest when initiated within 3 months of onset.

Spinal Cord Injury

For patients with incomplete SCI (AIS C/D), exoskeleton-assisted overground walking provides the high-intensity task-specific repetition needed to drive spinal neuroplasticity. A 2022 multicentre RCT (n=128, 6 hospitals) showed 43% of the exo group regained independent standing versus 11% in controls at 12 weeks.

Chronic Musculoskeletal Conditions

Patients with osteoarthritis, degenerative disc disease or post-surgical rehabilitation benefit from load-sharing assistance that enables earlier, pain-free mobilisation. Multiple Sclerosis & Neurodegenerative Conditions

Fatigue is the primary mobility barrier in MS. Exoskeleton assistance reduces the metabolic cost of walking by an average of 31% (Carpinella et al., 2023), allowing patients to sustain longer therapy sessions and maintain functional independence further into disease progression.

// Engineering Rationale

WHY THESE COMPONENTS

Every design decision is traceable to a specific clinical requirement. Here is the evidence behind each major choice.

Sensing — Why EMG over position control?
Surface EMG

Position-controlled exoskeletons impose a fixed movement pattern regardless of the patient's own motor output. This produces passive limb movement — mechanically correct, but neurologically passive. The central nervous system does not reorganise in response to passive movement at the same rate as active, intent-driven movement.

Surface EMG captures residual voluntary muscle activation — even a signal too weak to produce useful movement is sufficient to drive the controller. This keeps the cortical motor loop active throughout therapy, which is the primary mechanism of neuroplastic recovery after stroke and SCI.

Structure — Why aluminium-carbon composite?
Frame Material

Titanium offers superior strength-to-weight but is significantly more expensive and harder to machine for rapid fit adjustment — a critical requirement in a clinical environment where therapists resize the device multiple times per day across different patients.

6061-T6 aluminium provides 90% of the structural performance at 30% of the cost, with excellent machinability for the tool-free adjustment mechanism. Carbon fibre reinforcement is applied selectively at the three highest-stress zones identified through finite-element analysis — the hip joint bracket, the knee pivot and the pelvic attachment plate — where fatigue failure was observed in the prototype phase.

Power — Why lithium-ion over tethered power?
Onboard Battery

Tethered power supplies eliminate battery management but restrict the patient to a fixed area, preventing the overground, community-environment walking tasks that are most clinically meaningful. Ecological validity — practising in environments that resemble real-world use — is a well-established predictor of functional transfer after rehabilitation.

The 148 Wh LiNiMnCoO₂ cell chemistry was selected over LiFePO₄ for its higher energy density (enabling the slim dorsal pack profile) at the cost of slightly reduced cycle life — an acceptable trade-off for a clinical device that is regularly serviced. The hot-swap mechanism was driven directly by therapist feedback: any configuration requiring shutdown during a session was rejected at the design stage.

Intelligence — Why on-device AI vs. cloud processing?
Edge AI Controller

Cloud-based processing introduces round-trip latency of 40–120 ms depending on network conditions — well above the 30 ms threshold for transparent human-robot interaction. Any perceptible lag between the patient's motor intent and the joint assist disrupts the sensorimotor feedback loop and degrades the therapeutic signal.

All inference runs on the 4-TOPS edge NPU embedded in the controller board. The patient-specific model (≈ 2.4 MB) fits entirely in on-chip SRAM, ensuring deterministic 18 ms latency regardless of connectivity. Session data is logged locally and synchronised to the clinical dashboard over Wi-Fi between sessions — not during them.

// Design Philosophy

BUILT AROUND THE PATIENT

Five principles that governed every engineering and clinical decision from prototype to CE mark

Rehabilitation technology fails when it optimises for the wrong thing — impressive laboratory metrics that do not translate to ward usability or patient outcomes. Every component decision in the Nexus EXO was evaluated against a single question: does this measurably improve the patient's recovery trajectory?

01
Neuroplasticity First

The device must support, not replace, the patient's own motor effort. Every control algorithm, every sensor choice and every assistive mode is designed to keep the patient's cortical motor system engaged. A passive device that moves the limb perfectly but bypasses voluntary effort is, from a neurological recovery standpoint, of limited therapeutic value.

"The brain reorganises in response to what it is asked to do — not what is done to it." — Principle from the Nexus Clinical Advisory Board, 2021
02
Dose Over Technology

The most robust predictor of motor recovery after neurological injury is repetition dose — the number of task-specific practice trials. A simpler device that enables 2,500 steps per session consistently is more therapeutically valuable than a sophisticated device that averages 800 due to donning complexity, calibration time or patient discomfort. Complexity was removed wherever it did not directly contribute to dose delivery.

03
Therapist as Expert

The exoskeleton is a tool that amplifies the therapist's clinical expertise — it does not replace clinical reasoning. The adaptive AI recommends assistive levels and flags anomalies, but every parameter can be overridden in real time by the therapist. The dashboard was co-designed with 34 physiotherapists across three countries to ensure it surfaces clinically relevant information without cognitive overload.

04
Ward-Ready by Default

Many rehabilitation robots are technically impressive but operationally impractical: 20-minute setup times, specialised rooms, two-person donning, proprietary maintenance contracts. The Nexus EXO was designed from day one for a standard physiotherapy gym with no infrastructure modifications. Four-minute donning, hot-swap battery, standard disinfectants, and a device that fits in a standard storage cupboard.

05
Evidence Before Claims

No clinical claim in this documentation is made without a citation to a peer-reviewed source. Where the evidence is preliminary (e.g. Parkinson's disease) this is explicitly stated along with the ongoing trial reference. We believe that transparency about what is proven, what is promising and what is unknown is a prerequisite for clinical trust — and for patient safety.

06
Total Cost of Ownership

The acquisition price of an exoskeleton is the smallest component of its 10-year total cost. Maintenance contracts, consumables, training time, downtime costs and reimbursement rates dominate the financial picture. Nexus EXO is designed for low maintenance burden (no hydraulic fluids, no proprietary tooling), standard clinical consumables, and comes with a 3-year parts and labour warranty as standard with all fleet agreements.

// Clinical FAQ

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Common questions from rehabilitation teams, procurement departments and clinical leads

Absolute contraindications include severe osteoporosis (T-score < −3.5 without medical review), active lower limb fracture or surgical wound, uncontrolled spasticity (Modified Ashworth Scale ≥ 4), severe orthostatic hypotension, and active deep vein thrombosis in the lower limb.

Relative contraindications requiring physiotherapy assessment include significant lower limb spasticity (MAS 3), significant lower limb oedema, pressure sores at device contact points, BMI > 40, and height outside the 155–195 cm adjustment range. A full pre-prescription assessment checklist is included in the clinical user manual and training programme.
The competency-based certification programme takes 3 hours for physiotherapists and 1.5 hours for physiotherapy assistants. It covers device assembly and storage, patient assessment and prescription criteria, donning and doffing procedure, session setup and AI parameter adjustment, emergency stop and manual override, cleaning and maintenance, and documentation requirements.

Training is delivered by a Nexus clinical application specialist on-site (included in the purchase agreement) and supplemented by an online learning module and competency assessment. Re-certification is recommended every 2 years. All training records are logged in the Nexus clinical portal for CQC and JCI audit purposes.
United Kingdom: NHS England recognises exoskeleton-assisted gait training under the Rehabilitation Medicine Best Practice Tariff. Several Integrated Care Systems have established exoskeleton pathways with cost-per-QALY modelling available from our health economics team.

Germany: Covered under §302 SGB V for post-stroke and SCI rehabilitation in certified rehabilitation centres (Reha-Einrichtungen). DRG grouping available upon request.

France: Référentiel d'actes innovants hors nomenclature (RIHN) pathway active. Two regional health agencies have approved budget lines for fleet procurement.

United States: CPT codes 97110, 97530 and 97542 are applicable. Value-based care and bundled payment arrangements under CMS have supported adoption in 23 health systems. Prior authorisation templates available for major payers.

Our health economics team can provide site-specific ROI modelling and payer engagement support as part of the procurement process.
The device is CE marked and FDA cleared for use with complete SCI patients in passive assist mode, where the controller provides a pre-programmed gait pattern without requiring EMG input. In this configuration, the primary therapeutic goals are cardiovascular conditioning, bone density maintenance, spasticity management, bowel and bladder function improvement, and psychological wellbeing rather than motor recovery.

For complete SCI, the evidence base for motor recovery via exoskeleton therapy is significantly weaker than for incomplete SCI. We do not make motor recovery claims for AIS A/B patients, and the clinical protocol and patient information materials clearly communicate this distinction. Passive assist mode is also used during the initial assessment session for any patient where EMG signal quality is insufficient to trigger the intent-based controller.
The aluminium-carbon frame and all hard surfaces are IPX4 rated and compatible with alcohol 70%, Clinell Universal Wipes, Tristel Duo, and Sani-Cloth CHG 2%. The garment liner (worn directly against the patient's skin) is single-patient or machine washable at 60°C with standard NHS/hospital laundry detergent. A replacement liner set costs approximately £45 and is stocked by all major medical supplies distributors.

Full decontamination between patients takes approximately 4 minutes. Detailed IPC (infection prevention and control) guidance is provided in Section 7 of the clinical user manual and has been reviewed by infection control leads at three NHS trusts.
The device is designed with fail-safe passive compliance: in the event of any power or software fault, all joint actuators immediately release to a zero-torque back-driveable state. The patient's limbs move freely under gravity and their own muscle activity — no joint is locked or fixed in position.

A large physical emergency release button is located on the pelvic unit and releases the entire pelvic support harness within one second. All therapists are trained to perform a full manual emergency doff in under 20 seconds as part of the certification programme.

The fault management system logs all incidents and transmits them to the Nexus post-market surveillance portal in compliance with EU MDR Article 87 mandatory incident reporting requirements.
The adaptive controller monitors three real-time signals that indicate patient fatigue or deteriorating performance: EMG signal amplitude decay (muscle fatigue), stride symmetry index decline (compensatory movement patterns emerging), and patient-reported effort score (captured via a single-button input on the handle).

If any two of these signals cross pre-set thresholds within the same 5-minute window, the AI automatically increases assistive torque to maintain the quality of movement — rather than allowing the patient to continue practising poor movement patterns under fatigue, which can reinforce maladaptive compensations.

The therapist receives a notification on the tablet and can choose to accept the AI recommendation, override it, or end the session. Session notes are automatically flagged for review in the next session planning summary.
